Abstract    :
                This project investigates cycles of inter-ethnic conflict.  The argument is
              that anti-government protests by minorities generate a cycle of contention
              involving collective action processes -- counter-mobilization, bandwagons,
              external patronage, and intra-community competition -- that affect the
              likelihood of inter-ethnic conflict and cooperation.  The PIs apply this theory
              of inter-ethnic relations to the case of Blacks and Jews in the United States. 
              While conventional wisdom indicates that Black-Jewish relations have
              deteriorated in recent years, systematic description and explanation is
              virtually absent.  This work examines Black-Jewish interactions in New York
              City from 1900-1997.  It codes data from a variety of news sources and uses
              ARIMA intervention estimation and hazard models to test the model.  The
              research contributes to our understanding of two established fields -- social
              movements and Black protest.
