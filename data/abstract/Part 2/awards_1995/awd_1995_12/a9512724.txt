Abstract    :
              Coordination and Learning in Market Entry Games  In non-cooperative games with
              multiple equilibria, a fundamental question is what causes players to expect
              each other to implement a specific equilibrium. Stated differently, it is
              important to understand what cues, in the context of the game, lead players to
              focus or anticipate one equilibrium outcome out of the multiplicity, and thus
              play so as to make their anticipations self-fulfilling.  This study examines
              the possibility that in iterated games, coordination on a specific equilibrium
              may result through learning.  To isolate the role of learning, other previously
              identified means of producing coordination (e.g., pre-play communication,
              equity considerations, and environmental cues) are controlled for.
