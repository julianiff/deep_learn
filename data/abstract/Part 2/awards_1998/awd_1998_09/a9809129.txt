Abstract    :
                This doctoral dissertation research examines variations in land tenure systems
              in sub-Saharan Africa.  The study focuses on land rights reforms in Mali and
              Niger in the period 1970-1997.  It is hypothesized that the preferences of
              leaders to establish land rights declines when the revenue from resources
              exceeds a certain threshold; and that leaders have the greatest opportunities
              to realize profits in mineral-rich economies with poorly diversified productive
              sectors.  These hypotheses are tested using computer simulations, archival
              research, interviews and statistical tests.  This study contributes to the
              literature on property rights in anthropology, economics and political science
              and provides important insights for transition governments seeking to change
              their systems of property rights.
