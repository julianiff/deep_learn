Abstract    :
              The focus of this dissertation research is a comparative analysis of three
              memorial landscapes dedicated to the Civil Rights Movement: Atlanta,
              Birmingham, and Selma.  By comparing the contexts in which they were produced,
              the authoritative representations they offer, and visitor interpretations of
              them, this research probes the relationship between historic memory and social
              identity in the built environment. The study involves three tasks: archival
              research and interviews, an analysis of the interpretation and presentation of
              history, and a survey of visitor profiles and reactions. The proposal is set in
              a theoretical context of landscape interpretation and black history tourism
              (and heritage tourism more generally), and attempts to examine the place of
              race and racism in the U.S.  The results will elucidate the diversity of
              positions that characterize contemporary racial politics.
